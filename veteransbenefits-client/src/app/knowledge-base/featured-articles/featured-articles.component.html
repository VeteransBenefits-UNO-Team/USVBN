The Rating Schedule Detailed information on various benefits

Important Notice

Caution: The misuse of this schedule is strictly prohibited and may constitute a legal offense. It is intended to facilitate a deeper understanding of the criteria used by the VA in evaluating conditions. We encourage you to use this resource to gain insights into the assessment process and to articulate your condition more effectively.

Please Note: This schedule is not a checklist for self-diagnosis or representation. Its purpose is solely informational and educational.

Given the extensive nature of the content, we have organized this schedule into various system categories and sub-components for ease of navigation and comprehension. You are welcome to review the complete rating schedule and M21-1 documents; however, you may find our structured summaries more accessible and user-friendly.

The information provided here is based on the latest rating schedule as of November 2021.

Analogous and Equivalent Disabilities

Due to the the sheer volume and duplication of symptoms that the human mind and body can have, it is impractical for the rating schedule to have a rating for every single disability by name. In such cases that the Veteran's disability/disease/condition is not mentioned by name in the rating schedule, it will be rated under the most comparable rating.

Take for instance, there is only a rating for migraine type headaches in the schedule. If the Veteran has a diagnosis of headaches, the VA will rate under the migraine schedule.

Convalescent Rating (Temporary 100% Rating)

Veterans recently discharged from hospitalization may qualify for a temporary 100% rating under specific conditions. This rating generally applies for 1 to 3 months, with possible extensions up to 6 months. Further extensions may be granted based on individual evaluations.

Eligibility Criteria

  • The surgery or procedure must be linked to a service-connected condition.
  • Eligibility includes at least one month of convalescence or in cases of severe postoperative complications such as unhealed surgical wounds, recent amputations, or the necessity for extended immobilization.
  • Immobilization by cast, without surgery, of one major joint or more, also qualifies.

Following the convalescence period, a reevaluation of the Veteran's disability status will occur, unless a minimum rating is pre-determined by the schedule or the Veteran already has a 0% rating.

Veterans may be eligible for Special Monthly Compensation (SMC) S during the convalescence period. If the rating schedule specifies a temporary 100% rating for a certain duration (e.g., 6, 12 months), an additional month of 100% rating is granted, extending the period to 7, 13 months, respectively.

Veterans eligible for service connection retroactively during their convalescent period may file for back pay. Extensions of the temporary 100% rating due to prolonged recovery can be requested with medical evidence from a healthcare professional.

Filing for Temporary 100% Rating

Veterans can file for the temporary 100% rating with the assistance of a Veterans Service Officer (VSO) or by using VA Form 21-536ez. In cases where the surgery was performed at a VA facility, the VA is responsible for initiating the claim process. Consequently, the Veteran may not need to file themselves.

Hospitalizations (Temporary 100% Rating)

Veterans hospitalized for 22 or more continuous days at a VA hospital, or an approved facility, due to a service-connected condition are eligible for a 100% disability rate. This rate is retroactively applied from the day of admission.

The 100% rating is maintained until the end of the month in which the Veteran is discharged. However, for hospitalizations extending six months or more due to a mental disorder, the 100% rating continues for six months post-discharge, followed by a reevaluation to assess the current level of disability.

Depending on their existing disability ratings, Veterans may qualify for Special Monthly Compensation (SMC) S during their hospitalization.

Filing for Temporary 100% Rating Due to Hospitalization

Veterans can initiate a claim for the temporary 100% rating with the help of a Veterans Service Officer (VSO) or by submitting VA Form 21-536ez. In instances where the Veteran is hospitalized at a VA facility, the VA typically manages the claim process, potentially obviating the need for the Veteran to file.

Extra Scheduler

In exceptional or unusual circumstances in which the Veteran has factors such as marked interference with employment or frequent periods of hospitalization, the VA Director of Compensation Service may grant a rating that is higher than what is provided for in the rating schedule. Do know, these cases are exceptionally rare!

Incarcerated Veterans

Veterans incarcerated for more than 60 days in a Federal, State, or local penal institution due to a felony conviction will experience a reduction in their disability compensation.

Reduced Pay Structure

  • For Veterans rated 20% or higher: Compensation is adjusted according to 38 USC § 1114(a), which currently equates to $123.
  • For Veterans rated at 10%: The payment is reduced to half of the standard 10% rate.

Apportionment Process

In cases where a Veteran's spouse or dependents are dependent on the Veteran's disability compensation, they may request an apportionment. This means they can appeal for a portion, or all, of the reduced compensation to be redirected to them, based on demonstrated financial need.

Additional Notes

  • Veterans receiving Special Monthly Compensation (SMC) will not receive these payments while incarcerated.
  • If a Veteran’s conviction is overturned on appeal, their disability compensation, minus any apportioned amounts, will be reinstated.

Legal Protections for Veterans' Disability Ratings

Veterans are entitled to legal protections regarding their disability ratings, which vary based on the duration the rating has been held. These protections do not apply in cases of fraud, but it's important to note that administrative errors by the VA are not considered fraud.

5-Year Protection Against Reductions

A Veteran's disability rating that has been in place for 5 continuous years cannot be reduced solely based on a single C&P exam or records review showing improvement. If improvement is noted, a subsequent reevaluation may occur. Continued improvement may lead to a proposal for reduction in the rating.

10-Year Protection for Service Connection

After maintaining a service-connected disability rating for 10 continuous years, the connection cannot be severed, except in cases of proven fraud or incorrect service records. This protection stands even if the condition improves or if the Veteran is eligible for a rating increase or secondary service connection.

20-Year Total Protection

Once a Veteran has held a disability rating for 20 continuous years, the rating is completely protected and cannot be reduced under any circumstances.

Other Important Protections

  • Amputation Rule: If a Veteran undergoes a non-service connected amputation of a service-connected part, the original disability rating will continue unchanged.
  • Changes in the Rating Schedule: Veterans are grandfathered under the older rating schedule when changes occur. If improvement is detected under the old schedule, the rating may be adjusted according to the current schedule. Ratings are evaluated based on the schedule in effect on the effective date of the grant. A staged evaluation may apply if the new schedule is more favorable.
  • Age Consideration (Over 55): Commonly misunderstood, being over 55 does not provide rating protection. However, it generally exempts Veterans from regular reexaminations, except in specific cases like post-surgery evaluations or conditions like cancer. Filing a secondary claim may prompt a reevaluation of the primary condition.
